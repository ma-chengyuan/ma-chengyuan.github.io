Contemplating the "Language Policy" of This Blog

All English articles on this blog can be found here.

I believe writing this down here will help save me the struggle each time I am about to write.

There is a problem that has long troubled me since I decided to turn this site from a place where I post competitive programming solutions to a general-purpose blog:

Should I write my posts in Chinese, or would it be better to write them in English?

I can come up as well as foresee a lot of reasons for and advantages of writing English posts, to name a few:

  1. I can write technical articles in English, and writing them more often makes me better at it, so why not?
  2. As I am now at MIT and will look for internship / UROP positions in the U.S., writing in English allows me to show my work to potential employees, faculties or collaborators in addition to my CV.
  3. If I want to show off my work, writing in English could cater to a broader audience.

On the contrary, the benefits of writing in Chinese are in fact, not that obvious and imminent to me:

  1. While Chinese is my native language, I don't feel any significant reduction in difficulty in technical writing. I know many terms and expressions in English first. If I write in English, then all I need to do is to type my class notes word by word, but if I write in Chinese, I have to translate, and if I can't find any existing translation of a term, I will have to coin one myself (this is 80% the case when I'm writing PACT notes on approximation algorithms).
  2. From a more technical point of view, Chinese characters do not display well in MathJax-rendered equations, and there's no public interface to configure the fonts used in MathJax.

It seems that writing in English is undoubtedly a better choice.

(The best choice, of course, is to write all posts in both languages, but it would be tedious and time-consuming)

That said, you now see only a few English posts on this blog.

Partly this is due to my obsession that there should be one dominant language for a site. When I started to consider the language problem, I had already written ~20 posts. It would be weird to see a blog with its first 20 articles in one language and the rest in another. Consequently, I choose to follow what I call "linguistic inertia" and continue writing in Chinese.

Another reason is my hope that my writing in Chinese could help enrich the content in the Chinese technical community. I am often shocked while googling by the lack of “good” technical materials in Chinese. The coverage of the Chinese technical community simply does not match with the large population of passionate IT workers in China! There are a lot of quality Chinese documentation for popular frameworks and software (a.k.a. what people are paid to deal with), but there aren't many materials besides that. When I looked for how to set up a decompilation workspace for Minecraft, 0 Chinese articles popped up. When I searched for specific academic topics, such as approximation algorithms, I found no Chinese notes or posts except for some books. Sometimes when I do find some articles, some of them are just homogenous copies. To make things worse, many of them are poorly type-set, with fuzzy images and all the math and notations awkwardly typed in plain text -- such torture for my LaTeX-cultivated eyes that I often ended up reading some English sources they cite or other English posts instead. Though I hardly doubt anyone who deals with algorithms and computers would have difficulty reading English, it is still not a pleasant experience of being forced into reading a foreign language. I believe people would undoubtedly be happier if their curiosity could be fulfilled and their problem solved by some article in their mother tongue -- at least this is what happened to me. So I decide to make a change, starting with my blog. This is a major reason I insist on writing many posts here, such as those about the technical analysis of Minecraft Iceboat or PACT lecture notes, in Chinese, and I would be more than delighted if anyone could learn from these posts.

I have persuaded myself to write all my blog posts in Chinese with these two reasons above for years, but this language struggle wasn't wholly over. As I am writing my CV and wonder if I should include a link to this site, I must admit that referring Americans to a Chinese site may not be that helpful -- if you happen to be one of them, my apologies :)

Now that I come to think about it again. Perhaps there is no point insisting on one language for all posts. Why can't English posts and Chinese posts co-exist on my blog? Compromising my single-language OCD will save me the struggle of determining a dominant language for my blog, so I should probably do that.

That said, I still have to determine which language to use for each of my new posts. I want to do some casework where I can divide the topic of my posts into these categories:

  • Lecture/class notes: I will write them in Chinese. Because
    1. There are most probably English versions out there already.
    2. Their translation is tedious, but it does eliminate the shortcut of verbatim copying and force me to understand every bit in the lecture.
    3. It does give one some sense of achievement to be a "pioneer" -- to boldly translate what no Chinese has translated before!
  • Short notes for solving computer problems I encountered: I will write them in Chinese because I am usually just writing as a reference for myself in the future (and for others as well if possible). Many of these notes aren't that original since, in many cases, I was replicating solutions from other blogs and documenting what worked for me. I think I'll write in whatever I'm more comfortable with.
  • Some original thinking and explorations (such as the NTTP post and the technical analysis of Minecraft Iceboat): I might choose to write in either language, depending on my mood, the temperature that day, my heart rate, the moon phase that day, etc.
  • Emotional complaints: in Chinese, of course.

These categories are still subject to changes as I haven't 100% made up my mind yet. But I believe writing this down here will help save me the struggle each time I am about to write.
