Contemplating the "Language Policy" of This Blog

As the application season is drawing near, persuaded by my parents and anxious myself, I have been increasing the weight of foreseeable short-term benefits in my decision-making. Hence I think it's time that I should re-contemplate on which language I write my blog in, and write that down.

All English articles on this blog can be found here.

This is a problem that has long troubled me since I decided to turn this site from a place where I post competitive programming solutions to a general-purpose blog:

Should I write my posts in Chinese, or it would be better if I write it in English?

I can come up as well as foresee a lot of reasons for and advantages of writing English posts, to name a few:

  1. I can write technical articles in English, and writing them more often makes me better at it, so why not?
  2. It is almost certain that I will apply for U.S. and perhaps U.K. colleges. From a utilitarian point of view, if I want to give admission officers a chance to know what I'm doing, English seems to be the only choice.
  3. If I want to show off, writing in English could cater to a wider audience.

On the contrary, the benefits of writing in Chinese are in fact not that obvious and imminent to me:

  1. While Chinese is my native language, I don't feel any significant reduction in difficulty when it comes to technical writing. I know many terms and expressions in English first. If I write in English then all I need to do is to type my class notes word by word, but if I write in Chinese I have to translate, and if I can't find any existing translation of a term I will have to coin one myself (this is 80% the case when I'm writing PACT notes on approximation algorithms).
  2. Admission officers from Chinese universities don't, and don't need to care about my blog posts at all. So from a utilitarian perspective, writing in Chinese doesn't give me any short-term benefit. Interestingly enough, I did receive an email from a headhunter (perhaps I should start creating my linkedin profile now? :D )
  3. From a more technical point of view, Chinese characters do not display well in MathJax-rendered equations, and there's no public interface to configure the fonts used in MathJax.

It seems that writing in English is certainly a better choice, isn't it?

(The best choice, of course, is to write all posts in both languages, but it would be really tedious and time-consuming)

That said, not a single English post have I written on this blog.

Partly this is due to my subjective obsession that there should be one dominant language for a site, and because when I started to consider the language problem I have already written ~20 posts and it would be really weird if you see a blog which has its first 20 articles in one language and the rest in another, I choose to follow what I call "linguistic inertia" and continue writing in Chinese.

Another reason is my hope that my writing in Chinese could help enrich the content in Chinese technical community. I am often shocked, while googling, by the lack of good technical materials in Chinese. The content of the Chinese technical community simply does not match with the large population of passionate IT workers in China! There are a lot of quality Chinese documentations for popular frameworks and software (a.k.a. what people are paid to deal with) though, but there aren't many materials beside that. When I looked for how to setup a decompilation workspace for Minecraft, 0 Chinese articles popped up. When I searched for some specific academic topic, such as approximation algorithms, I found no Chinese notes or posts except for some books. And in case when I find some articles, some of them are homogenous copies and many of them are poorly type-set, with fuzzy images and all the math and notations awkwardly typed in plain text -- such a torture for my LaTeX-cultivated eyes that I often ended up reading some English sources they cite or other English posts instead. Though I hardly doubt anyone who deals with algorithms and computers frequently would have difficulty reading English, it is still not a pleasant experience of being forced into reading a foreign language and I believe people would certainly be happier if their curiosity can be fulfilled and their question solved by some article in their mother tongue -- at least this is what happen to me. So I decide to make a change, starting from my own blog. This is a major reason why I insist on writing many posts here, such as those about the technical analysis of Minecraft Iceboat or PACT lecture notes, in Chinese, and I would be more than delighted if anyone can learn from these posts.

I have persuaded myself to write all my blog posts in Chinese with these two reasons above for years, but this language struggle wasn't really completely resolved. Furthermore, as the application season is drawing near, persuaded by my parents and anxious myself, I have been increasing the weight of foreseeable short-term benefits in my decision-making. Hence I think it's time that I should re-contemplate on which language I write my blog in, and write that down.

Now that I come to think about it again. Perhaps there is no point insisting on one language for all post... Why can't English posts and Chinese posts co-exist on my blog? Compromising my single-language obsession will save me the struggle of determining a dominant language for my blog, so I should probably do that.

That said, I still have to determine which language to use for each of my new post. I would like to do a case work here can divide the topic of my posts into these categories:

  • Lecture/class notes: I will write them in Chinese. Because
    1. There are most probably English versions out there already.
    2. Translation is tedious, but it does eliminate the shortcut of verbatim copying and force me to understand every bit in the lecture.
    3. It does give one a little sense of achievement to be a "pioneer" -- to boldly translate what no Chinese has translated before!
  • Short notes for solving computer problems I encountered: I will write them in Chinese, because I am usually just writing as a reference for myself in the future (and for others as well if possible) and many of these notes aren't really that original since in many cases I was just replicating solutions from other blogs and document what worked for me. I think I'll just write in whatever I'm more comfortable with.
  • Some original thinking and explorations (such as the NTTP post and the technical analysis of Minecraft Iceboat): I might choose to write in either language, depends on my mood, the temperature that day, the moon phase that day etc -- I haven't quite really decided yet.
  • Emotional complaints: in Chinese, of course.

These categories are still subject to changes as I haven't 100% made up my mind yet. But I believe writing this down here will help save me the struggle each time I am about to write. If you happen to be also reading this... never mind :)
